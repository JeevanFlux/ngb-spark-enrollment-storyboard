Section 1 of 12

Why we built it this way

The problem we're solving and how we framed it.

The Next Gen Benefits (NGB) enrollment redesign starts from one fundamental observation: most employees lack confidence and trust when picking benefits, especially new hires who've never had to choose between a PPO and an HDHP and don't know what either of those acronyms really means. The existing enrollment experience — both ours and what competitors ship — pushes jargon-heavy options at people and asks them to make consequential financial decisions in 6 wizard steps. That's the wrong frame.

Karen Stroup's executive direction was unambiguous on this: do not just replicate the same enrollment screens our competitors show. She wanted us to reimagine the experience, build in decision support, add an AI assistant that personalizes guidance, and stop forcing employees to decode benefits terminology to get to a good choice. Her own provocation, almost verbatim: "What if someone could speak or type to an AI assistant about their current health situation, needs, and risk tolerance, simulate different scenarios, and be guided to the best plan?"

That last word — "best plan" — is where we hit a hard constraint. WEX is not licensed to sell insurance. We can educate. We can engage. We can guide. We cannot recommend a specific plan as "best." Rachel Pedersen's compliance directive (May 6 meeting) was firm: every piece of language in the storyboard had to be reframed around educate, don't recommend. So when Wexley surfaces a plan match, we say "closest match to what you described", not "my recommendation". We show trade-offs. We let the user own the choice.

The throughline: Reimagine enrollment around a conversational AI that gathers context, surfaces the closest-match plan with the trade-offs laid out, simulates real-life scenarios on demand, and keeps every option transparent so the user owns every decision. Educate, don't recommend.

Section 3 of 12

The design principles we set

Five rules we held ourselves to as the storyboard came together.

1. One continuous conversation, not seven separate pages. Every frame in the storyboard is a moment in time within a single chat surface. The user never leaves — Wexley drops cards, summaries, comparisons, forms, and recap blocks inline as the conversation moves. This is the single biggest departure from the existing wizard-style enrollment.

2. Educate, don't recommend. Compliance constraint expressed as a writing rule. Plan match cards say "closest match to what you described." Wexley says "here's how this plan would handle your situation," not "this is the right plan for you." Trade-offs are always visible. The user always has the final call.

3. Show your work. When Wexley does something — drafts an HR email, surfaces a plan match, runs a scenario — the reasoning is visible. The "Why this fits you" section names Nicole's specifics ("Maya is a toddler", "you take a daily inhaler"). The scenarios show line-item bills. The peer benchmark spells out where the data comes from. Nothing is a black box.

4. Adapt to the person, not the plan catalog. Wexley names Maya by her age. It deliberately matches Vision Basic (not Premium) because Nicole didn't mention glasses needs — proving Wexley won't reflexively up-sell. The flow skips HSA when Nicole's on a PPO instead of just hiding it. Personalization isn't decoration; it's the load-bearing piece.

5. Mobile-first, voice-friendly. Most enrollments happen on phones. Talking beats thumb-typing. Voice mode is a top-tier input on Frame 1, not a hidden affordance. Live transcript is shown so the user can verify what was heard.

Phase 1 walkthrough · Getting to know you

Frames 1 through 6, plus Branch A (edit) and Branch B (HR request). The opening conversation where Wexley gathers context.

1

Home — a guided enrollment experience with Wexley

enrollment_home_v3.html
Open frame ↗
Why we designed it this way

The page itself is the start of enrollment. There's no "Click Enroll Now to begin" wizard entry — instead the magenta-bordered Wexley input is the primary CTA. We made it conspicuous (not hidden) because the conversational flow only works if users actually start the conversation. Seven starter chips below ("I take meds daily", "First time enrolling", etc.) reduce blank-page friction and seed the model with useful context up front.

Technical dependencies

LLM HRIS SSO

Wexley needs an LLM behind the chat input (any modern model — GPT-4 class, Claude, etc.) with a system prompt scoped to enrollment. The starter chips are not LLM intents — they're hardcoded prompt seeds. Acme HR data (name, DOB, dependents) is pulled from the HRIS via SSO before this page loads, so Wexley already knows who Nicole is.

1b

Wexley listening — voice-first input state

enrollment_home_v3_voice.html
Open frame ↗
Why we designed it this way

Most enrollments happen on mobile. Talking beats thumb-typing for the kind of context Wexley needs ("I have a 2-year-old, take a daily inhaler, want predictable costs"). Speech-to-text Audio not stored

Real-time speech-to-text API (Whisper, Google Speech, or platform native). Audio is processed in real time, not stored — only the transcript persists. This is called out explicitly in the frame's privacy panel because it matters to users.

Spousal plan comparison — should Alex be on Acme's plan?

screen3_f_spousal_compare.html
Open frame ↗
Why we designed it this way

This is Discovery Slides North Star feature #2 — dual-household modeling. We put it before the medical match because the answer changes who Acme's plan needs to cover. LLM intent classification SBC parsing (pending) Provider network search (pending)

The 4-option question card uses LLM intent classification to interpret Nicole's free-text answer if she types instead of tapping. SBC parsing is pending — needs OCR + LLM extraction of deductible/OOP max from a PDF. Provider-network search is pending — needs per-carrier API integration.

Medical match — hero card

screen3_a_medical_recommendation.html
Open frame ↗
Why we designed it this way

The hero plan-match moment. Section 10 of 12

Technical wiring · how the AI piece actually works

What's hardcoded, what's LLM-driven, and what backend services this all depends on.

What the LLM is doing

The LLM (any modern model: GPT-4 class, Claude, etc.) is doing four jobs throughout the experience:

  • Intent extraction from free-text and voice input — turning "I take meds daily for asthma" or "first real job with benefits" into structured context (daily meds: yes; meds list: asthma inhaler; experience level: new).
  • Personalized response generation — Wexley's utterances are LLM-generated using the current file-panel state and conversation history. The "toddlers usually have well-child visits" parenthetical isn't hardcoded; it's the model adapting to Maya's age.
  • Email and document drafting — Branch B's HR email, the network-check question Alex sends his HR, the email-a-copy subject line.
  • Custom scenario generation (Frame 10b) — the most ambitious LLM task. Parses a user's free-form what-if, maps it to procedure codes, looks up costs, calculates plan-by-plan out-of-pocket, and generates the narrative output.
What's NOT LLM-driven

Plenty of the experience is deterministic, and that's the right call:

  • Plan match ranking — a deterministic rules engine using the user's stated context crossed with each plan's coverage. The LLM generates the rationale ("Why this fits you") but doesn't pick the plan.
  • Tax savings calculations — IRS rules + tax-bracket lookup. No room for LLM creativity here.
  • IRS dependent rules — full-time-student suppression for toddlers, etc. Hardcoded conditional logic.
  • Compliance language guardrails — never use "recommend" or "best plan" in user-visible output. Implemented as a post-generation filter, not just a prompt instruction.
"Intent buttons" — how the chips work

The starter chips on Frame 1 ("I take meds daily", "First time enrolling", "I have a young kid", etc.) and the in-flow quick-replies ("Yes, all good", "Edit something", "What if my year goes badly?") are not LLM intents in the technical sense. They're hardcoded prompt seeds — tapping one inserts a pre-written message into the conversation as if Nicole had typed it. This is intentional: it gives users a low-friction path to start, while keeping the underlying flow predictable.

The free-text path goes through real LLM intent classification. If Nicole types "actually, my husband already has insurance through his job", that triggers the spousal-coverage flow even though no chip was tapped.

External APIs and data sources

This is what needs to be wired up for the experience to ship:

  • HRIS integration (read + write) — name, DOB, gender, marital status, address, dependents. SSO authentication. Per-field metadata on locked vs. editable.
  • Plan data from carriers — Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for every plan Acme offers. Premiums, deductibles, OOP max, copays, coinsurance, network type, prescription tiers.
  • Procedure cost data — national-average costs by CPT/HCPCS code (HCCI, FAIR Health, or similar). Drives the scenarios and custom-scenario branches.
  • Provider network search — per-carrier API to verify Maya's pediatrician (Dr. Patel) is in-network. Currently flagged "Check needed" — this is pending.
  • Tax data — federal + state tax brackets, IRS contribution limits, federal Dependent Care Tax Credit phase-out tables.
  • Speech-to-text — Whisper, Google Speech, or platform-native. Audio not stored.
  • Marketplace partner catalog — Acme's negotiated provider list with paycheck-deduction integration into WEX payroll.
  • Mail send — for the HR email drafting (Branch B), the network-check question, and the email-a-copy output. Either server-side send or mailto: handoff.
PHI and privacy guardrails

This is the boundary that shaped a lot of design decisions:

  • No claims data. We can't pull individual claims into the experience. Plain-English context Nicole tells us is the substitute.
  • No persistent user profile beyond the session. Wexley operates on session context only — what Nicole tells us today, plus HRIS data, plus her plan elections at the end. No longitudinal tracking.
  • Peer benchmarking uses aggregate industry-standard data only. The "What people in similar situations tend to do" panel never references individual user data. The disclaimer says this explicitly.
  • SSN encrypted in transit and at rest. Only goes to the insurance carrier, not stored in the conversational session.

How to use this in your demo

Practical guidance for the Friday team demo and the Karen review.

Open with the "why". Don't dive into screens. Start with: Karen's confidence-and-trust brief, the compliance constraint that forced "educate, don't recommend", and the four hypotheses we're proving (AI decision support, voice, marketplace, peer benchmarking). Get reviewers to nod that the framing is right before showing any frames.

Walk the journey, but pause on the hero moments. The whole storyboard is 26 frames. Don't walk all 26. The hero moments are: voice mode (Frame 1b), Branch B HR email drafting (the strongest "AI in workflow" demo), the spousal compare (Frame 7a, North Star feature #2), the medical match with personalized rationale (Frame 7b), the custom scenario generator (Frame 10b — Karen's literal provocation), the marketplace inline (Frame 14d), and Review (Frame 16) with the employer-contribution stat. Spend 1–2 minutes on each. Click through other frames quickly.

Be explicit about what's pending. Reviewers will ask "is this real or mockup?" The honest answer: the storyboard is static HTML. FigmaMake interactive prototype is next. SBC parsing, provider-network APIs, and synthetic-user testing are all pending. Don't overclaim.

When the inevitable "but what if..." comes up, lean on the design principles. The "educate, don't recommend" rule answers a lot of compliance pushback. The "show your work" rule answers black-box concerns. The "adapt to the person" rule answers "this feels generic" critique. The principles aren't hand-waving — they're load-bearing decisions with sources.

Don't apologize for what's missing. The storyboard is exploring a direction, not specifying a build. Phrasings to use: "this is what the experience could feel like if we lean all the way in," "this is a candidate flow we'd want to test with synthetic users next," "this is the path we're proposing — pushback welcome."
