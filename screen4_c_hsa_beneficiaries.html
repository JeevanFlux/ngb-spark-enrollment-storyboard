Why this matters

Your HSA is the only spending account with money you actually own. FSA, DCFSA, HRA — none of those have inheritable balances. HSA does. So if you pass away with $3,500 sitting in your HSA, the IRS needs to know who gets it. Without a beneficiary on file, it goes to your estate (which can mean probate, delays, and taxes).

Tax implication you should know: If your spouse is your beneficiary, they inherit your HSA tax-free — they keep using it like their own. If anyone else is your beneficiary (parents, kids, friends), they inherit it as taxable income the year you pass. That's why I default to spouse when there is one.

What "Primary" vs "Contingent" mean

Primary: the person who inherits first. Contingent: the backup, who only inherits if the Primary can't (deceased, refuses, etc.). Most people just pick a Primary; contingent is optional but recommended for older couples or anyone with complex family situations.
